The gift album worth more than any other album.

Why your grandmother needs a digital family tree, and why it may be the best gift you ever give her.

Three months ago, I sat with my grandmother in her living room and asked her what her mother was called.

"Rosa," she answered immediately.

"And Rosa's mother?"

She paused for a moment. "Chaya. I think. Maybe Hanna."

"And Chaya's mother?"

A long silence. My grandmother, a razor-sharp 84-year-old woman, looked at me and gave me a sad smile. "I don't know, sweetheart. That part we already lost."

## The moment I realized we are losing a family

I am 38. In 20 years, my children will ask me the same questions. And I will tell them "Rosa," and "Chaya, maybe Hanna," and somewhere there the story will break. Three generations back, and suddenly the tree ends. Not because the family ended, but because no one took the time to write it down.

It is not because we do not care. It is because the photo album stays in a drawer. Grandpa's video tapes from Dad's bar mitzvah remain on VHS that no one can play anymore. The stories Grandma tells at Friday night dinner stay at Friday night dinner, and none of us takes out a phone and records them.

Family history does not disappear in one explosion. It evaporates slowly, grandmother after grandmother.

And that is exactly what we built Toldotay for.

## Wait, just another genealogy app?

No.

I know that when you hear "family tree" you think about MyHeritage or Ancestry.com. Apps where you upload DNA results, search archives, and discover that you have a third cousin in Argentina.

That is not what Toldotay does. And that is not what Toldotay is competing with.

Our competition is:

- A digital photo frame: great, but it shows one image at a time and without context.
- A memory book in Q&A format: beautiful, but static, and once it is printed, the story stops.
- A photo album in the closet: that one needs no explanation.

Toldotay is something else. It is not a tool for researching a family. It is a place where the family stays alive.

## How it actually works: three people, three roles

This is the story of how an average family uses Toldotay. Three characters. Three roles. No technical explanation needed.

### The buyer: a 45-year-old daughter

Her name is Michal. Her mother, Rachel, is turning 75 in a month. Michal already gave her a bracelet last year, a watch the year before, and she is tired of searching for something "special" that will be forgotten a week later.

She signs up for Toldotay. Within ten minutes, she builds the skeleton of the tree: mother, father, grandfather, grandmother, and her own two children. She uploads four photos. That is it. The skeleton is ready.

She receives a printable QR code and wraps it with a short note.

On the birthday itself, Michal places the QR code on the refrigerator in her parents' home during the family meal. Anyone who scans it enters the tree. Aunt Ruth from Australia adds a photo of her mother at age 20. A cousin from Montreal corrects Grandpa Yaakov's date of death. A 16-year-old nephew uploads a selfie with his grandmother.

Within a week, Rachel's tree grows from 7 people to 43. Everyone contributes. No one has to manage it. Michal does not need to chase anybody.

### The gift recipient: Rachel

Rachel receives a tablet, one possible option in the gift package, with a Toldotay icon on it. The screen opens to a display mode, not an "app."

It rotates automatically between:

- A photo of her granddaughter from last week
- A short biography of her mother, generated with our AI from the information Michal entered
- A reminder: "Today is your cousin Avi's birthday. He is 52."
- A branch of the tree: three generations leading up to her

Rachel does not have to tap anything. The tablet sits on a shelf in the living room like a photo frame, but with a family that moves.

## Let’s be honest: Toldotay did not start this way

We began with a vision of a "living family encyclopedia": a tool for researchers of rabbinic lineages, for people building complex genealogies, and for those searching for a cousin from the nineteenth century.

We built serious infrastructure: AI that writes biographies in Hebrew with live Google search, complex permission management, multitenancy, automatic Hebrew dates, and alerts for broken family branches.

And then we tried to sell it.

We found what every startup eventually discovers: the people who truly need this tool do not pay for it. The people who are willing to pay do not actually need that version of it.

Genealogy researchers already pay MyHeritage or work with community registrars. They are not the target audience for a new SaaS product.

But then we noticed something else. The people who signed up for the beta were not genealogists. They were sons and daughters between 35 and 50 who said things like, "I want to build this for my mother before it's too late," or "I want my children to know my grandfather who died before they were born."

This was not research. It was care. And people pay for care.

So we pivoted. Everything we built is still there: the AI, the dates, the RBAC, the visualization. But the product is no longer just a "genealogy tool." It is a living creation.

## Why now, specifically?

Two things changed over the last decade that make this product relevant today in a way it simply was not before:

1. **Our grandparents know how to use tablets.** The grandparents of 2026 are not the generation that feared computers. They use WhatsApp, watch YouTube, and some of them even post online. A tablet in the living room no longer sounds futuristic; it is already normal.
2. **AI can now write biographies.** My grandmother knows her father. She knows less about her grandfather. And her great-grandfather? Not really at all. But if he was a known rabbi, a Hasidic leader, or even just a Jewish immigrant from a certain aliyah, Gemini 2.5 Pro with live Google search can write a paragraph in Hebrew about him. Not from DNA, but from sources: Wikipedia, memorial books, and Jewish genealogy sites. That was simply not possible two years ago.

## A personal note to close

After I finished speaking with my grandmother that evening, I opened Toldotay and added the rest of the family. I added a "?" next to Chaya because I did not know for sure. I added a photo of Grandma that I found in an old album.

Two months later, my aunt, my father's sister, whom I had not spoken to in years, entered the tree and updated Chaya's mother's name: Hanna. Hanna Zonnenfeld. Born in Plonsk in 1898.

Five generations back. In 20 years, when my children ask me, I will know how to answer. Because I acted in time.

This gift is not only for your grandmother. It is also for yourself, 30 years from now.
